Reading Success in Arabic

Many foreign language textbooks include reading passages that are written specifically for students. Such passages use only the vocabulary and grammatical structures that the students have already learned. Unfortunately, reading such passages will not fully prepare you for reading texts written for native Arabic speakers, in which even advanced students of Arabic will encounter words and possibly even grammatical structures with which they are unfamiliar. Al-Kitaab fii Taallum al-Arabiyya includes passages that were written for native speakers in each chapter. These passages are often challenging, given that they contain words and grammatical structures you've never seen before. Research suggests that students who are successful at reading this type of material use appropriate strategies before, during, and after reading to help them understand these unfamiliar items--or at least read around them. Less successful second-language readers generally fail to learn how to use strategies. This activity and others to follow are designed to help you acquire skills in using successful strategies.


Before Reading

The most valuable thing in helping foreign language students to understand unfamiliar material is context.


It looks like this is an announcement page for marriages.Look at the article on page 6. What information can you gather about the context of this article before actually reading it?
Important Note before you look at page 6: The three lines of text in the book in English contain spoilers and information about the articles. Although using these questions will certainly help you create a context that will help you better understand the Arabic on this page, resist the temptation to look at the English (for a minute). When you progress to reading material outside of a textbook you won't have such questions to steer you in the right direction. Get in the habit of trying to figure things out on your own, without resorting immediately to such a "crutch." You won't always have a "crutch" so get in the habit of relying on strategies that you can use in any setting.
  Example


From the layout and the pictures, I would say that this is an announcement page of some sort. Each picture has a man and a woman, and a couple of them are clearly wedding pictures. I'd say they are wedding announcements from a newspaper or magazine.How did you gather that information?
  Example

If you arrived at a similar conclusion as the example answer above, you employed an effective pre-reading strategy or strategies. Then go back to the article and see what additional information you can gather.

briefly skim the text before reading
identify the purpose of the article (i.e. advertisement, news article, financial report, announcement, etc.)
identify the type of discourse (i.e. essay, outline, poetry, list, etc.)
try to recognize common phrases and patterns
try to understand the title
read bold, underlined, or otherwise important-looking material
look for things that are repeated or numbered
look at pictures or other visual clues to help you make an informed guess about the nature of the passage
